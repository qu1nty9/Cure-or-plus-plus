\begin{abstract}
Object-recognition systems are often evaluated on clean or synthetically corrupted images, but practical image use also includes acquisition artifacts, digital transfer, recompression, screenshots, and channel changes. CURE-OR++ builds a compact, reproducible benchmark layer around CURE-OR for measuring how vision models fail under object-centric native challenge conditions and real transfer pipelines. On the Full-CURE-OR v0.4 probe, eight usable baselines cover CLIP/OpenCLIP zero-shot models and frozen-feature prototype classifiers. The strongest current level-5 baseline, DINOv2 ViT-S/14 Prototype, reaches only 0.2766 mean level-5 accuracy despite 0.7520 clean accuracy. Across all eight usable baselines, grayscale salt-and-pepper noise, salt-and-pepper noise, and grayscale gaussian blur collapse to near-chance accuracy, with all models at the floor threshold. Confidence analysis shows that stronger zero-shot models can remain substantially overconfident under severe native challenges: OpenCLIP ViT-B/16 DataComp XL reaches 0.1451 level-5 accuracy while retaining 0.4997 mean confidence. A type-10 grayscale control confirms that grayscale conversion alone is damaging but does not explain the full native level-5 collapse. A 180-output real-transfer v0.2 block covers messenger upload/download, phone screenshot/resave, and video-call frame capture; source-matched drops are moderate, with video-call frame capture producing the largest mean drop at 2.1 percentage points. The open-weight VLM track extends this guardrail to a 900-row v0.3 prompt pack with 100 clean rows and 800 real-transfer rows over four app/device pipelines. Across seven completed open-weight VLMs, LLaVA-OneVision Qwen2 7B leads with 0.9775 real-transfer accuracy, Qwen2.5-VL-7B reaches 0.9613, InternVL3-2B reaches 0.9600, and Qwen2.5-VL-3B exposes a generation-instability failure mode with 0.7650 real-transfer accuracy and a 0.2088 unparseable rate. Three hosted OpenAI provider rows now run on the 210-row provider prompt pack: GPT-5.4-mini reaches 0.9611 real-transfer accuracy, GPT-5.4 reaches 0.9556, and GPT-5.5 reaches 0.9500 after targeted retries to eliminate hidden-reasoning empty outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Robustness benchmarks often study isolated corruptions such as blur, noise, compression, or exposure shifts. Real image use is frequently more operational: images are captured, resized, recompressed, screenshotted, shared through applications, restored, or viewed through video-call pipelines. CURE-OR++ is designed around that narrower operational question: how do object-recognition baselines fail under reproducible object-centric challenge and transfer conditions?

This draft focuses on the Full-CURE-OR v0.4 controlled probe, the evaluated real-transfer v0.2 validation block, executed open-weight VLM prompt-pack rows, including a 900-row v0.3 VLM extension, and the first hosted-provider VLM rows. The current repository also contains a smaller CURE-OR++ v0.1 Kaggle-ready artifact. The real-transfer and VLM blocks reduce the main external-validity gap, but should still be interpreted as guardrails rather than a comprehensive deployment or frontier-assistant study.

\paragraph{Current contributions.}
The current artifact provides:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a manifest-driven Full-CURE-OR v0.4 probe over clean rows and native challenge rows;
  \item a multi-family baseline comparison covering CLIP/OpenCLIP zero-shot models, a stronger DataComp XL OpenCLIP variant, and four non-CLIP prototype classifiers;
  \item a level-5 consensus failure analysis across eight usable baselines;
  \item confidence and calibration analysis for the usable CLIP/OpenCLIP-family zero-shot rows;
  \item a type-10 grayscale control and paired color-versus-grayscale challenge-family analysis;
  \item an evaluated real-transfer v0.2 guardrail for messenger, screenshot, and video-call pipelines;
  \item a 210-row VLM prompt-pack path with executed open-weight SmolVLM2-500M, SmolVLM2-2.2B, InternVL3-1B, InternVL3-2B, LLaVA-OneVision 0.5B, Qwen2.5-VL-3B, Qwen2.5-VL-7B, and LLaVA-OneVision 7B rows;
  \item a 900-row open-weight VLM v0.3 extension over four app/device transfer pipelines;
  \item three hosted-provider OpenAI VLM rows with response auditing, retry handling, and zero unparseable merged outputs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Benchmark Design}

CURE-OR++ uses manifest-driven evaluation. Each row links a source image, output image, object label, challenge family, recipe, severity, and metadata. This keeps aggregate results traceable to well-defined image groups and makes the benchmark easy to audit.

The Full-CURE-OR v0.4 track uses 500 clean probe rows and 38,999 native challenge probe rows across official challenge types 02-09 and 11-18. It also includes a type-10 grayscale no-challenge control. The real-transfer v0.2 track pins 30 clean mini-CURE-OR sources, three pipelines, two repeats per source and pipeline, and 180 collected transferred outputs. Collector-supplied metadata identifies iPhone 15 Pro as the capture device, WhatsApp as the messenger pipeline, and FaceTime as the video-call pipeline.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
All reported native CURE-OR means are computed from aggregate prediction rows and weighted by row count. The level-5 consensus analysis ranks each official challenge type by mean severe-condition accuracy across usable baselines, then reports both floor counts and cross-model rank stability. The real-transfer block is evaluated with a source-matched protocol: each transferred output is compared against the same 30 clean source images used to create the real transferred samples. Confidence and calibration metrics are reported for the CLIP/OpenCLIP-family zero-shot rows where model probabilities are comparable under the shared prompt protocol.

\paragraph{Statistical treatment.}
Real-transfer confidence intervals use source-level bootstrap resampling over the 30 selected source images. This intentionally treats repeated transferred outputs from the same source as dependent measurements rather than as independent test examples. The intervals are therefore wide, and the real-transfer result is interpreted as an external-validity guardrail rather than as a precise ranking of small app-specific effects.

\paragraph{Release boundary.}
The repository tracks code, configs, aggregate reports, paper tables, and generated figures. Raw CURE-OR images, local real-transfer payloads, per-image prediction dumps, and source-dataset archives remain excluded from Git and are governed by the upstream dataset terms.

\paragraph{VLM/API track.}
The repository also defines a separate multiple-choice visual QA prompt pack over the same real-transfer source set. This track is intentionally kept outside the CLIP/OpenCLIP/prototype leaderboard: VLMs produce text responses, have provider-specific versioning, and require answer extraction and raw-response auditing. The initial prompt pack contains 210 image-question rows per model, covering 30 clean source images and 180 transferred outputs. The larger v0.3 prompt pack contains 900 rows per model, covering 100 clean rows and 800 real-transfer rows over messenger upload/download, phone screenshot/resave, social-app resave, and video-call frame capture. The executed open-weight rows use \path{HuggingFaceTB/SmolVLM2-500M-Video-Instruct}, \path{HuggingFaceTB/SmolVLM2-2.2B-Instruct}, \path{OpenGVLab/InternVL3-1B-hf}, \path{OpenGVLab/InternVL3-2B-hf}, \path{llava-hf/llava-onevision-qwen2-0.5b-ov-hf}, \path{Qwen/Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct}, \path{Qwen/Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct}, and \path{llava-hf/llava-onevision-qwen2-7b-ov-hf} on Kaggle GPU with the Hugging Face runner and response evaluator. The hosted-provider rows use OpenAI GPT-5.4-mini, GPT-5.4, and GPT-5.5 through the Responses API with \texttt{store=false}; raw provider response files remain local, while sanitized aggregate tables and audits are tracked.

\subsection{Level-5 Robustness}

The strongest current level-5 row is DINOv2 ViT-S/14 Prototype: 0.7520 clean accuracy, 0.4393 native mean accuracy, and 0.2766 native level-5 mean accuracy. The strongest zero-shot contrastive row is OpenCLIP ViT-B/16 DataComp XL: 0.5460 clean accuracy, 0.2561 native mean accuracy, and 0.1451 native level-5 mean accuracy. Stronger features and pretraining improve the leaderboard, but they do not remove the severe native challenge collapse.

\subsection{Consensus Failure Ordering}

The top three consensus level-5 failures are grayscale salt-and-pepper noise, salt-and-pepper noise, and grayscale gaussian blur. All eight usable baselines are at the floor threshold on these three challenges. Pairwise level-5 rank correlations range from 0.892 to 0.988, suggesting a stable severe-challenge hardness core rather than arbitrary per-model failure lists.

\subsection{Confidence and Calibration}

The confidence pass currently covers the usable CLIP/OpenCLIP-family zero-shot rows. OpenCLIP ViT-B/16 DataComp XL reaches 0.1451 level-5 accuracy while retaining 0.4997 mean confidence. Its worst level-5 overconfidence case is grayscale salt-and-pepper noise, with 0.0100 accuracy, 0.7393 mean confidence, and a 0.9760 high-confidence wrong rate. Severe native challenge failure can therefore preserve high confidence.

\subsection{Grayscale Control}

The type-10 grayscale no-challenge control is damaging but does not explain the full level-5 collapse. For OpenCLIP ViT-B/16 DataComp XL, grayscale control accuracy is 0.3908 while native level-5 mean accuracy is 0.1451. Paired channel-effect analysis also shows that every usable model is worse on grayscale level-5 native challenges than on paired color level-5 native challenges.

\subsection{Real-Transfer Validation}

Real-transfer v0.2 evaluates 30 clean mini-CURE-OR sources across 10 labels, three real pipelines, and two repeats per source/pipeline. The source-matched report compares each transferred output against the same 30 clean source images. The collection used an iPhone 15 Pro, with WhatsApp for messenger upload/download and FaceTime for the video-call/video-transmission pipeline. Effects are moderate rather than catastrophic: video-call frame capture has 79.6\% mean real accuracy and a 2.1 percentage-point mean drop, messenger upload/download has 80.0\% mean real accuracy and a 1.7 point drop, and phone screenshot/resave has 82.5\% mean real accuracy with a -0.8 point mean drop. This supports using real-transfer v0.2 as an external-validity guardrail for the larger simulated and native CURE-OR analysis.

\subsection{Open-Weight VLM Prompt-Pack Runs}

To validate the assistant-style VLM path without relying on paid provider APIs, we first ran eight open-weight VLM rows on a 210-row prompt pack using Kaggle GPU. That pilot established the generation runner, answer extraction, and raw-response audit path. We then promoted the track to a 900-row v0.3 prompt pack with 100 clean rows and 800 real-transfer rows per model. The Kaggle environment resolved to a Tesla P100, so the notebook pins a P100-compatible CUDA PyTorch stack and runs generation in float16 with model-specific memory controls where needed.

\input{../reports/vlm_open_weight_full_v03_paper_table.tex}

The v0.3 VLM table is the main assistant-style benchmark block in the current draft. LLaVA-OneVision Qwen2 7B is the strongest completed open-weight row, reaching 0.9775 real-transfer accuracy with zero unparseable responses. Qwen2.5-VL-7B is also strong at 0.9613 real-transfer accuracy, while Qwen2.5-VL-3B exposes a separate generation-stability failure mode: it remains usable on clean rows but produces many unparseable transferred-image outputs. The InternVL family comparison is more nuanced than a simple scaling result. InternVL3-2B improves aggregate real-transfer accuracy over InternVL3-1B, but it has a larger clean-to-real drop and a weaker video-call frame-capture row. SmolVLM2-2.2B remains competitive with the smaller InternVL rows, and LLaVA-OneVision 0.5B provides a lower-capacity LLaVA contrast with zero unparseables but a concentrated weakness on the \texttt{dymo\_label\_maker} label.

Across completed open-weight rows, video-call frame capture is the dominant transfer stressor, appearing as the hardest pipeline for six of seven models. The exception is SmolVLM2-2.2B, whose hardest pipeline is social-app resave. This supports treating the VLM block as a real-transfer audit of both recognition accuracy and generation stability rather than as a single leaderboard score.

\section{Related Work}

CURE-OR++ should be positioned as a compact, object-centric robustness artifact rather than a replacement for broad VLM robustness suites. Nearby benchmarks include R-Bench~\cite{rbench}, which studies real-world corruptions for large multimodal models; MLLM-IC~\cite{mllmic}, which benchmarks multimodal models against image corruptions; VLM-RobustBench~\cite{vlmrobustbench}, which aggregates many corrupted settings over standard multimodal benchmarks; and MMD-Bench/CLEAR~\cite{mmdbenchclear}, which studies degraded-image understanding and restoration-oriented training. These works are broader than CURE-OR++ in model scope, task scope, or corruption coverage.

The intended differentiator is not scale, but transparent generation, paired metadata, small object-recognition scope, and failure tables that are easy to inspect by object class, challenge recipe, severity, and model family. The VLM/API track should therefore be interpreted as a bridge to modern multimodal assistants rather than as the central benchmark contribution of the current artifact.

\section{Limitations}

Real-transfer v0.2 is small: 30 sources, three pipelines, and two repeats per source/pipeline. It should not be treated as broad real-world transfer coverage or as a precise estimate of app-specific effect sizes. Full-CURE-OR v0.4 is a controlled probe rather than an exhaustive evaluation of every source image. Prototype classifiers and zero-shot contrastive models are useful robustness contrasts, but they are not identical evaluation protocols. Confidence and calibration analysis currently covers the usable CLIP/OpenCLIP-family zero-shot rows rather than every prototype row. The VLM/API track includes eight 210-row open-weight assistant-style rows, seven 900-row open-weight extension rows, and three hosted-provider OpenAI rows; it is still not a broad multi-provider frontier comparison. Results should therefore be read as object-recognition robustness evidence rather than a complete multimodal assistant benchmark.

\section{Conclusion}

CURE-OR++ currently provides a strong pre-paper benchmark artifact. The Full-CURE-OR v0.4 probe shows severe native challenge failures across model families, consensus floor failures under salt-and-pepper and blur variants, and confidence-preserving errors in stronger zero-shot models. Real-transfer v0.2 adds a small external-validity guardrail: real app and device pipelines produce moderate, model-dependent effects rather than universal collapse. The completed SmolVLM2, InternVL3, Qwen2.5-VL, and LLaVA-OneVision prompt-pack runs validate the open-weight VLM evaluation path. The 900-row v0.3 extension turns that path into a usable assistant-style benchmark block, with strong 7B rows, small-model family contrasts, and a concrete generation-instability failure case. The OpenAI provider rows add hosted-assistant comparison points and confirm that the same prompt, parser, and audit protocol can be applied to external VLM APIs.
